Coelenterata (Cnidaria): Diagnostic Features and Classification Up to Class with Examples

Coelenterata, also known as Cnidaria, is a phylum of simple aquatic animals. They are primarily marine organisms known for their radial symmetry and specialized cells called cnidocytes, which contain stinging organelles (nematocysts). These organisms exhibit two body forms: the polyp (sessile) and the medusa (free-floating).

Diagnostic Features

  • Habitat: Mostly marine, with a few freshwater species.
  • Body Symmetry: Radial symmetry.
  • Body Structure: Diploblastic (two germ layersâ€”ectoderm and endoderm).
  • Coelom: Acoelomates (without a body cavity).
  • Cnidocytes: Specialized cells containing nematocysts for capturing prey and defense.
  • Nervous System: A simple nerve net without a brain.
  • Digestion: Extracellular and intracellular digestion within the gastrovascular cavity.
  • Reproduction: Both asexual (budding) and sexual reproduction; life cycle may include alternation of generations.

Classification of Coelenterata (Cnidaria)

1. Class Hydrozoa

  • Features: Alternation of polyp and medusa forms.
  • Examples: Hydra, Obelia, Physalia (Portuguese man-of-war).

2. Class Scyphozoa

  • Features: Predominantly medusa form; polyp stage reduced or absent.
  • Examples: Aurelia (jellyfish), Rhizostoma.

3. Class Anthozoa

  • Features: Only polyp stage present, no medusa stage.
  • Examples: Sea anemones, corals, Gorgonia (sea fan).

4. Class Cubozoa

  • Features: Box-shaped medusa with complex eyes and potent venom.
  • Examples: Chironex fleckeri (box jellyfish).

Summary

Coelenterates are aquatic animals characterized by radial symmetry, a simple body structure with two layers, and the presence of cnidocytes. They exhibit diverse forms and life cycles, ranging from the sessile polyp to the free-floating medusa. These animals play essential ecological roles in marine ecosystems, such as forming coral reefs.
